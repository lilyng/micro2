HOW TO be an Ally

Being an ally is more than being sympathetic towards those experiencing discrimination or just simply believing in equality. Allies support marginalized groups of people to ensure they are valued. This can entail speaking up for members of another group who are being treated unjustly through both intentional and unintentional actions from other people in pursuit of ending oppression and creating equality.



AWARENESS AND EDUCATION
Not sure where to start? Try having conversations with individuals that you want to know more about or taking a self-inventory of your own values/experiences or simply conducting personal research. The more informed you are, the more confident you will feel in standing up in future situations where microaggressions occur.

OPEN AND SUPPORTIVE ENVIRONMENT
Treat every situation with respect. Listen actively with empathy when someone reaches out to you without judgement.

ACTION
Using your own discretion of the situation context, speak up when you witness a microaggression. One recommended communication strategy is the OTFD approach (Open The Front Door). Observe: Concrete, factual, and observable (describe what you see clearly) Think: Thoughts based on observation (state your thoughts) Feel: Emotions (express your feelings). Desire: Specific request or inquiries about desired outcome.
Example: "I've noticed there's been lot of interruptions during our all-hands meeting. I feel uncomfortable that the new workers are not able to share their thoughts. Can we figure out some meeting discussion guidelines so everyone has a say and we can be more productive in future?"

MICROAFFIRMATIONS
Microaffirmations are small acts that promote inclusion, listening, and support for marginalized groups of people. In the workplace, this may entail acknowledging and commending the performance and achievements of workers.

Doing personal research on microaggressions and learning when to identify them is especially useful as an ally. Below are some resources to help you start:

Sample Scenarios & Responses



A stranger asks your friend: "So where are you really from?"

Assess the situation and see how your friend reacts. If your friend looks to you for help, you could say: "Are you asking where they grew up or where their ancestors are from?"


Everyone gets their grades back and your friend got the highest score. A classmate says: "She got that good grade because she's Chinese."

Try changing the focus onto your friend's work ethic and accomplishments rather than her ethnicity. You could say: "She got that grade because she worked hard. She did such a great job!"


You pick a seat between two girls in class. One of the girls is biracial. The girl on the left says: "You're mixed right? Biracial people are so exotic."

Try being friendly and starting a conversation with the girl on the right. You could also say: "Saying exotic might make her feel alienated. Try to be more respectful."



Your workplace just hired a new worker, you overhear a coworker say: "Black people have no right to take our jobs."

Actions speak louder than words. Set a role model example for the rest of your coworkers by being friendly to the new worker. You could also say: "We've been working here for awhile, so we should be welcoming."



Your friend is ordering food and the waiter tells them, "Wow, I didn't expect your English to be so good."

If your friend looks upset by the waiter and turns to you for help, you could say: "Of course her English is good. She was born and raised in the United States. English aside, we would like to order now."


A new student joins your group and points out to one of your friends: "You're not like other African Americans I've met."

If the student follows your group, you could say: "I'm sure you meant that as a compliment, but it's not respectful to judge someone based on your past experiences with other people."



A bilingual foreign exchange class joins your Japanese Language class. One students asks aloud: "So what do you guys speak back in your country? Asian?"

See how the student reacts. If the student looks troubled, you could say: "Asian isn't a language. Japanese and English are though."



Someone approaches your friend and asks: "Is your hair real? Can I touch it?"

Respect how your friend responds since it's their personal space and body. If your friend looks upset and looks towards you for help, you could say: "No you cannot, please be respectful. I wouldn't want someone touching my hair."


A classmate is giving a presentation. Someone whispers to you: "She's so articulate for a Latino person!"

You could redirect the conversation focus onto her skills rather than ethnicity: "She is very articulate! I want to learn from her, I'll ask her for a few public speaking tips."



You're at an amusement park ride line and the stranger behind you and your mom and asks: "So what are you guys?"

Focus on whichever activity you and your mom intended to do. If the stranger continues to persists, you could smile and say: "We're customers waiting in line. If you meant to ask where we live, we're from [town]."



You're shopping at the mall with your best friend. A guy approaches your friend and says: "You're really pretty for a dark skinned girl."

If you best friend looks uncomfortable, you could step in and say: "If you're trying to flatter her, it's not working. My best friend is beautiful, period."



You're sitting in the cafeteria with your friends and someone asks your Latino friend: "How could you not like tacos?"

You can continue your conversation with your friends. You could also speak up and say: "I wouldn't make assumptions. Everyone has different food preferences."
